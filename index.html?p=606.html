Internet Bank vs. Traditional Bank: Which is More Secure

When trying to decide on where to put your hard-earned money, the sky often appears as the limit. But considering a large number of banks out there, how do you know which one is the best for you?

Should you go for a traditional bank or an internet bank? Knowing how to choose the right banking facility can positively impact different aspects of your financial life. Therefore, you shouldn’t pick the first bank that comes your way. You have to analyze it carefully.

What Is Internet Banking?

Many banks offer online banking in addition to the general services offered at their brick and mortar facilities. Nonetheless, some banks have started operating online strictly. Great examples of this include those facilitating digital payments.

Such banks operate in the same manner as traditional banks, the only differences being that:

• Clients have to do everything online using a smartphone or computer
• The bank may only have one physical branch or none at all
• Account-holders have to use a debit card to withdraw or spend money
• Bank customers can deposit or make transfers using an app, ATM, or bank website

Some online-only banks also allow their clients to write paper checks. An internet bank like to boast about how simple it is for their clients to open an online account. Apart from being 18 years, applicants need to provide the following details:

• Full legal name
• Date of birth
• Residential street address if applying from the United States
• Tax identification number or Social Security number

The bank may require the applicant to submit a copy of their driver’s license or any other identifying document before making any digital payments. Apart from applying online, applicants can also do so via mail or phone.

How Is an Internet-based Bank Different from a Traditional Bank?

The main difference between an internet-based bank and a traditional brick-and-mortar bank is that online banks primarily offer online services. While you won’t get to meet a banker in person, you can access your account anytime using a computer or web-enabled mobile.

Both banks offer to store and help you manage your money. However, they do vary in terms of the available services, capabilities possessed by each, and the features offered by each bank. While the offerings at each banking facility may vary, the following are their general features:

Traditional/Typical Bank Features

• Brick and mortar branches are available
• In-person, online, and phone support
• Higher fees than online banks
• In-person and web-based processes
• Low-interest rates for clients operating savings accounts
• Mobile app and online access are usually available
• Additional fees when making digital payments

Internet/Online Bank Features

• Don’t have a physical location
• Online customer service, and in some cases over the phone
• Low or no costs when transacting
• Swift and easy online processes
• High-interest rates on checking and savings accounts
• Automatic mobile app and online access

What to Look for in a Bank/ Digital Payments Processor

Now that you know the differences between these two types of banking facilities, a time will come when you’ll need to choose one. Knowing what to look for in a bank or digital payments processor can save you a lot of heartache and stress in the future.

Before you begin your search, you have to understand that there’s no single account that’s right for you. Additionally, there’re lots of factors to consider, and selecting the right bank starts with understanding your personal and banking preferences.

Some of the factors to consider during your search for a bank include:

1. Security of Your Hard-earned Money

Whether traditional or internet-based, all banks must be insured by the United States Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation. If you prefer banking with a credit union, confirm that the National Credit Union Association insures it.
Don’t bank with a facility that doesn’t have this type of protection.

2. Customer Support Options Available

When you have a question or problem with your account, the last thing you want to deal with is an unresponsive customer support team. No one wants to sit on hold for hours or have to deal with a customer support representative who is arrogant or unhelpful.

Conduct a quick search online to see which facilities have the best customer support teams.

3. Banking Fees and Account Maintenance Fees

If possible, focus on finding a bank that will not charge you for conducting basic transactions on your account. Some of the fees you’ll want to avoid at all costs include monthly account fees, fees for account assistance, and per-check fees.
All these fees can make your banking experience expensive.

4. Minimum Balance Requirements Set by the Bank

Please inquire from the bank on whether you need to maintain a predetermined minimum balance before they can start charging you account fees. While this doesn’t apply to all accounts, some will automatically attract such fees.
If a minimum balance is present, confirm that it’s something that you can afford.

5. What Are the Applicable Interest Rates?

The interest rates charged by banks work both ways: the rates you’ll pay when borrowing via a loan or on credit and the rates the bank will pay when you make a deposit. Ideally, the rates on the deposits should be higher than the charges on your loans.

The Bottom Line/ Conclusion

So, which type of bank is better: the internet bank or the traditional bank? The answer is not as simple as you would like, as it depends on your needs. Opt for web-based banks if you want to enjoy low fees and high-interest rates.

On the other hand, if you want to enjoy the ability to make cash deposits and access large amounts of cash fast, stick to the traditional bank. Fortunately, you don’t have to choose during the early days, especially if you already have an account with a typical bank.

You can slowly ease into online banking by using the features provided by your traditional account. Additionally, just because you have opened an online account doesn’t mean you need to close out all your other accounts. Try to leave some money in both accounts.
