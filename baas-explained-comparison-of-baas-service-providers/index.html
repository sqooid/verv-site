Introduction

FinTechs have rapidly been changing the game in the payments industry over the past couple of years. The exponential growth of payment processors like Stripe and PayPal has played a significant role in driving many innovations in the payments industry, which has contributed to the huge market gains and incredible valuations.

As part of this growing trend in the payment processing industry is banking as a service (BaaS) and the pandemic acted as a great catalyst.

However, even with this growth in digital payments, the underlying question remains- just how much will such FinTechs disrupt customers’ banking choices existent for centuries? Yes, the changes are here already, but how does it affect service availability and reliability? Read on to learn more.

What is the difference between open banking and BaaS?

Open banking is very different from banking as a service in a digital payment system. The primary difference between these two is that banking as a service is a payments solution falling under open banking. Generally, it is a strategy banks and FinTechs use to work together to ensure that there is an integrated experience for banking customers.

When it comes to BaaS, third parties take advantage of the Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) to gain access to some of the already existing functions of a conventional bank. When they tap into such functionalities, they can provide enhanced, simplified or different services.

On the other hand, open banking is essentially the framework that makes it possible for BaaS to exist. Therefore, through open banking any payment solution will obtain market guidelines on how any third party can gain access to this consumer data, process it and use it.

The logic behind this in digital payments is that customers are the information owners. Therefore, as a result of some politically established regulations, this type of banking encourages more open markets to ensure they stay competitive.

How BaaS affects the consumer and the market

There are a plethora of FinTechs worldwide, which means the competition in the online payment industry is very stiff. In the UK alone, there are over 1600 payment solution providers, making it a major FinTech hub globally.

Such numbers are ideal for sustaining competition; this, spawning innovations. When you look at the UI/UX pricing, design models that explain different data sets and how they relate to saving and saving, you will see how such numbers are vital. When companies integrate API development, they essentially connect with conventional banks, they can easily download and process data.

Therefore, it only means that digital engagement is going to be critical. It may pose significant challenges for companies offering credit cards like Apple and Monzo. Using a credit card won’t make much sense if you can just you your face or thumbprint. This means different firms will have to coin ways to encourage customers to directly interact with their innovations. It all lies in the customer experience.

A Comparison of Banking as a Service Providers

Many FinTechs are rapidly adopting platform-based banking to enjoy significant benefits. Also, about 79% of bankers contend that BaaS has led to greater personalization of financial products and services while streamlining connections to other sectors.

The following are some of the best companies that are spearheading BaaS strategies and have first- hand experience of the advantages:

• Verv: This is a complete financial services suite in the digital payments industry that has been on the forefront in the adoption of BaaS strategies to ensure any online payment is smooth. That is why they offer simplified business onboarding, support recurring payments, offer custom modules for more specialization while ensuring that they offer payroll visibility and invoicing analytics. These are all designed to improve the overall customer experience.

• SolarisBank: Based in Berlin, this firm offers a completely digital Baas platform. They proudly hold a banking license that lets them collaborate with business partners from different parts of Europe. Their solutions include modular structure ensuring that there is selective and seamless integration of payment processing services tailored to meet specific business needs. You can easily access their payment cards, KYC and transactions through APIs.

• Bankable: This London-based firm provides a wide range of financial transaction solutions under the BaaS model. They are instrumental in empowering FinTechs to roll out innovative payment solutions that are cost and time efficient to help with technological and regulatory challenges.

• Starling Bank: Although not purely a BaaS platform, it is an retail bank that includes some BaaS functionality. It is through this bank that some banks and FinTechs can use their license to transfer and receive funds in real-time, including taking care of the regulatory considerations.

• Green Dot: This branchless bank’s BaaS platform was as a result of the Walmart partnership that enabled the unbaked to enjoy banking services. It has grown over the years to offer a robust infrastructure that can run banking programs and payments at scale. It also runs marketing and branding services.

Conclusion

More banks are now looking into the future and strategically planning of how they can use the rise of Internet of Things (IoT) to remain profitable while ensuring top-tier customer experience in the digital payments sector of the payments industry.

Inasmuch as these technological trends may sound a bit futuristic, the integration of BaaS platforms will set the pace for FinTechs.
